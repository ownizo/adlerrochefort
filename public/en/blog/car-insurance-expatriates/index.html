Car insurance for expatriates in Portugal: what you need to know

April 2026 · 8 min read

Portugal has become one of the most sought-after destinations for expatriates worldwide. With its mild climate, competitive cost of living and internationally recognised quality of life, thousands of foreigners choose Portugal to live and work every year. But when it comes to driving in the country, questions arise that may seem straightforward but hide significant complexities: how does car insurance work for foreigners? Can I use my driving licence? Does my claims history from my home country count?

This guide answers all these questions and provides the practical information you need to be legally protected while driving in Portugal.

Can I drive in Portugal with a foreign driving licence?

The answer depends on where your licence was issued:

  • EU/EEA driving licence: valid in Portugal without needing to be exchanged, as long as it is within its validity period. When it comes up for renewal, you must renew it at the IMT (Institute for Mobility and Transport) in Portugal
  • UK driving licence (post-Brexit): recognised for the first 90 days. After establishing residence, you must exchange your licence at the IMT within two years
  • Driving licence from countries with bilateral agreements: countries such as Brazil, Mozambique, Angola and other Portuguese-speaking nations have agreements that facilitate the exchange. Check with the IMT for specific deadlines
  • Driving licence from other countries: recognised temporarily (up to 185 days for tourists). After establishing residence, you may need to exchange your licence, which may require theoretical and/or practical exams
The validity of your driving licence is an essential requirement for car insurance. A policy taken out with an invalid licence can be voided by the insurer in the event of a claim.

Is car insurance mandatory for expatriates?

Yes, without exception. The obligation to have motor third-party liability insurance applies to all vehicles with Portuguese registration, regardless of the owner's or driver's nationality. The minimum mandatory capital in 2026 is:

  • Bodily injury: €6,450,000 per claim
  • Property damage: €1,300,000 per claim

If you brought your vehicle from another EU country, the insurance from the country of origin is valid in Portugal as long as the vehicle retains its original registration. However, when you register the vehicle in Portugal (mandatory after establishing tax residence), you will need to take out insurance with an insurer operating in the Portuguese market.

Documents required to take out car insurance in Portugal

Portuguese insurers require specific documentation to underwrite a policy. As an expatriate, you should prepare the following documents:

  • Identification document: valid passport, citizen's card (if you have one), or residence permit
  • NIF (Tax Identification Number): essential for any contract in Portugal. You can obtain it at the Tax Office or through a fiscal representative
  • Valid driving licence: original and, if not in Portuguese, English or Spanish, a certified translation
  • Vehicle Registration Document (DUA): the vehicle's registration document, issued after legalisation or purchase in Portugal
  • Proof of address: utility bill, rental agreement or bank statement
  • Claims experience letter: a document issued by your previous insurer, proving your claims-free driving history (essential for obtaining discounts)

No-claims bonus transfer: how to make your driving history count

One of the biggest challenges for expatriates when taking out car insurance in Portugal is the loss of the driving history accumulated in their home country. Without proof of claims-free experience, insurers apply the base tier (no discount), which can significantly increase the premium.

The good news is that many Portuguese insurers accept transferring the no-claims bonus from foreign insurers, provided you present adequate documentation:

  • Claims experience letter: issued by your previous insurer, stating the number of claims-free years. It should be in English or accompanied by a certified translation
  • No-claims bonus declaration: if applicable, a document proving your bonus tier in the country of origin
  • Claims history for the last 5 years: some insurers request a detailed record of the last five years

Not all insurers in Portugal accept this transfer on the same terms. Some recognise up to 50% of the bonus accumulated abroad, while others apply more restrictive rules. This is where the intermediation of a specialist broker becomes particularly valuable.

Request your claims experience letter from your previous insurer BEFORE cancelling the policy. Once cancelled, obtaining this document can be more time-consuming and bureaucratic.

Imported vehicles: registration and insurance

Many expatriates bring their vehicle from their country of origin. The registration process in Portugal involves several stages, each with implications for insurance:

  • Vehicle Customs Declaration (DAV): if the vehicle comes from outside the EU, it must be declared at customs. EU vehicles are exempt from customs duties
  • ISV exemption (Vehicle Tax): expatriates who move permanently to Portugal may benefit from ISV exemption, provided they meet certain requirements (residence abroad for at least 12 consecutive months, among others)
  • Technical inspection: the vehicle must pass an inspection at an authorised centre to obtain Portuguese registration
  • Portuguese registration: after legalisation, the vehicle receives Portuguese plates and must be insured in Portugal

During the registration period, which can take weeks or months, the vehicle can circulate in Portugal with foreign plates and insurance from the country of origin, as long as it covers international circulation (Green Card). However, once Portuguese plates are issued, foreign insurance is no longer valid.

How much does car insurance cost for expatriates?

Car insurance for expatriates tends to be more expensive than for Portuguese drivers with a proven local track record. The main reasons include:

  • Lack of local history: without a bonus tier in the Portuguese system, the premium starts at the base rate
  • Unknown risk profile: insurers apply more conservative premiums when they cannot assess the driver's history
  • Area of residence: expatriates are concentrated mainly in Lisbon, Porto and the Algarve, areas with higher premiums

As a guide, the 2026 price ranges are:

  • Third-party liability (TPL): between €350 and €700 per year for an expatriate without a local bonus
  • Third-party plus: between €500 and €1,100 per year
  • Comprehensive cover: between €800 and €2,000 per year, depending on the vehicle's value

Transferring your no-claims bonus can reduce these amounts by 20% to 40%, demonstrating the importance of presenting proof of your driving history from your country of origin.

Common mistakes expatriates make when taking out car insurance in Portugal

In our experience with expatriate clients, we have identified patterns of error that repeat frequently:

  • Not requesting the claims experience letter before moving: this is the most important document for reducing the premium, and many expatriates only think of it once they are already in Portugal
  • Keeping foreign insurance after registering the vehicle: once Portuguese plates are issued, insurance from the country of origin is no longer valid. Driving under these conditions is equivalent to having no insurance
  • Not declaring all drivers: if other family members drive the vehicle, they must be included in the policy
  • Underestimating the importance of additional coverages: in an unfamiliar country, roadside assistance and legal protection are particularly valuable
  • Comparing prices without understanding the coverages: the cheapest insurance is rarely the most suitable, especially for those still adapting to Portuguese traffic and roads

Practical tips for expatriates driving in Portugal

Beyond having the right insurance, there are practical aspects every expatriate should know:

  • Electronic tolls: many motorways in Portugal use electronic tolls (Via Verde or licence plate reading system). Register with one of these systems to avoid fines
  • Roundabouts: priority in roundabouts in Portugal differs from many other countries. Vehicles already in the roundabout have right of way
  • Parking: in major cities, parking is regulated and often paid. Use apps such as ePark or Via Verde
  • Speed limits: 50 km/h in built-up areas, 90 km/h on national roads, 120 km/h on motorways
  • Documents required in the vehicle: you must always carry your driving licence, vehicle registration document (DUA), valid insurance certificate (Green Card) and inspection sticker
Driving in Portugal is generally a pleasant experience. The roads are modern and well signposted. But every country has its own particularities — adapting is the best way to protect yourself.
